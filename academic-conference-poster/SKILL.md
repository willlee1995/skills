# Academic conference poster (visual story)

## Core insight

A strong academic poster is **not** a mini paper on a wall. It is a **visual shortcut** so a passing viewer grasps the topic, key result, and reason to stop within seconds. Most posters fail by overloading text instead of guiding attention through a **simple visual story**.

## Principles

### Reading flow

- Use an **obvious reading path**: simple **column** structure or **clearly separated sections** so viewers know where to start and where to go next.
- **Overcomplicated layouts** make people abandon the poster because decoding feels hard.

### Figures over paragraphs

- Drive the poster mainly with **figures, graphs, and schematics**; keep explanatory text **small**.
- Poster-session audiences do not want dense paragraphs.
- **Cut words repeatedly** until only bare essentials remain.

### What to prioritize

- **Title**: easy to read, say, and understand at a glance—it invites the right audience. Prefer **simple language**; avoid fancy wording so a distracted attendee can process it quickly.
- **Conclusion / takeaway**: make it **rapidly visible**. Viewers often want the takeaway before detail: **main message obvious first**, supporting data around it.

## Design rules

- **Color palette**: keep it **limited**, ideally **~three colors**, for cohesion. Prefer palette tools: **one accent** plus a small set of **supporting** neutrals/backgrounds—avoid improvising many hues.
- **Templates**: do not lean too hard on **university poster templates**; they often favor **institutional branding** over clarity. If a logo is required, keep it—but **redesign** around simplicity, **visual hierarchy**, and **strong figures**.

## Good vs bad (quick tests)

- **Bad**: crammed words, visually overwhelming, hard to understand from a short viewing distance.
- **Useful test**: can someone **several feet away** still grasp what is going on?
- **Stronger**: simple colors, clearer flow, poster **dominated by visuals** not text blocks.
- **Highlighted pattern**: content in **three areas**, **one large** attention-grabbing visual, **minimal** wording.

## “Better poster” model

- Put the **main finding in large central text**; keep **supporting details at the sides**.
- Add a **QR code** for deeper material (methods, extra figures, paper)—matches real behavior: **scan quickly**, dive deeper only if hooked.

## Build checklist

Use when planning or reviewing a poster:

- [ ] **One-sentence takeaway first**—then build the poster around it.
- [ ] **Simple reading path** (columns or clearly boxed sections).
- [ ] **Replace paragraphs** with figures, graphs, schematics wherever possible.
- [ ] **Cut text aggressively** until only essential interpretation remains.
- [ ] **Short, plain-language title**—no jargon inflation.
- [ ] **~Three colors** in the palette.
- [ ] **QR code** (optional) for detail beyond the poster.

## Agent workflow

1. Restate the **one-sentence takeaway** and check it is **visually primary** (size/position), not buried.
2. Sketch or describe **reading order** (numbered zones or columns); flag **dead ends** or competing focal points.
3. For each text block, ask: can this become a **figure, chart, or schematic**? If not, **shorten** or **move to QR/supplement**.
4. Audit **title** for speakability and glanceability; audit **palette** for accent + neutrals.
5. Apply the **distance test** and **three-foot / few-seconds** clarity mentally or via described layout.

## Optional follow-up

If the user wants a **printable one-page template** (e.g., InDesign, PowerPoint, or PDF layout grid), propose a minimal grid after the takeaway is fixed—do not expand scope unless they ask.
